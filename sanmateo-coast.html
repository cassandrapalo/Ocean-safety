Beautiful days and calm-looking surf can hide powerful swells capable of sweeping people off rocks and beaches with no warning.

⚠️
Long-Period Swell Kills on Calm Days

The danger is not a rogue wave out of nowhere — it's a predictable pattern you can learn to read. Long-period swell arrives in sets, separated by long quiet intervals. People walk to the water during the calm and are swept in when the set arrives. Check the swell period. Watch the sets before you approach. A long wait between waves is not safety — it's the setup.

What is swell period — and why it kills?

Swell height alone doesn't tell the story. A 4-foot wave with a 20-second period carries exponentially more energy than a 6-foot wave at 8 seconds. Long-period swell travels from storms thousands of miles away — arriving silently on what looks like a calm day.

5–8sLocal wind swell
9–12sModerate risk
13–16sHigh risk
17s+Extreme risk

Check NOAA Buoy 46012 (Half Moon Bay) for current swell period before visiting any San Mateo County beach.

Rules That Save Lives
01
Check the Period First
Swell period is your warning. At 12+ seconds, sets arrive with long quiet intervals between them. At 15+ seconds, stay completely clear of the shore.
02
Watch Before You Move
Sit above the water line and observe for 15 minutes. Count the time between large waves. Learn the set rhythm before you go anywhere near the water.
03
A Long Calm Is the Danger
The sets that kill people arrive after the longest quiet intervals. If it's been calm for several minutes, stay back — do not walk toward the water.
04
Wet Rocks = No-Go
If rocks are wet, the ocean has already reached them during the last set. Stay above the wet line. The next set will reach the same place.
05
Cold Water Kills Fast
Pacific water here runs 50–55°F year-round. Cold shock and swimming failure happen within minutes of immersion.
06
If Swept In: Float
Don't fight the current. Float on your back, conserve energy, signal for help, and swim parallel to shore.
